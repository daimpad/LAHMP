## Profile 37 — Termites

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate in tropical, subtropical, and semi-arid agricultural deployment contexts where termite activity is ecologically present and measurable.

**Ecological role:** Termites are the dominant soil engineers in tropical and subtropical agricultural landscapes. Fungus-growing (Macrotermitinae) and grass-harvesting species drive nutrient cycling, water infiltration, and organic matter decomposition at rates that can exceed earthworm activity in equivalent temperate systems by two to three orders of magnitude. Mound-building species create persistent micro-habitat structures that persist for decades. Their activity is a direct indicator of soil restoration under agroforestry, no-till, and compost application practices.

**Relevant landscapes:** Sub-Saharan Africa (all savanna and sudano-sahelian contexts), Sahel, South and Southeast Asia (tropical cropland, agroforestry), drylands of the Middle East and North Africa. The Skoura M'Daz oasis (Morocco) and PK-17 Mauritania test cases are both within range.

**What is currently authored:** Identity block, ecological rationale, A3 services linkages, and Level 1–3 protocol names and output metrics are drafted. Level 2 and 3 seasonal windows and references are incomplete.

**What is missing:** A1 pressure linkages, A2 challenge linkages, B1/B2 practice linkages, B2 direction of change, Linkage C connected groups, A4 EFG relevance list. Level 2 and 3 seasonal windows. References for Levels 2 and 3.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 2–3 hours to complete A-block linkages and fill missing protocol fields. Recommended reviewer: soil ecologist with tropical agricultural systems experience.

## Profile 38 — Freshwater Turtles

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate where freshwater turtle species are ecologically present and regionally recognised as indicators of water body health. Requires confirmed presence of permanent water bodies (pond, river, irrigation canal, rice paddy system, or equivalent) within or adjacent to the monitored landscape.

**Ecological role:** Freshwater turtles are long-lived apex omnivores of agricultural water bodies. Their population structure (abundance, size-class distribution, sex ratio) integrates decades of water quality and habitat availability signals that shorter-lived species cannot provide. They are highly sensitive to pesticide loading, organic pollution, predation pressure from invasive species, and water body permanence. Where rice-paddy and irrigation canal networks are involved — as in many IUCN NBS Hub deployment contexts in Asia and Africa — turtle presence/absence is one of the most accessible indicators of water body ecological function for community monitors.

**Relevant landscapes:** Southeast Asia (rice-paddy systems, Vietnam VSA test landscape), South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa (permanent water bodies in dryland systems), Mediterranean (irrigation canals, oasis ponds including Skoura and similar dryland contexts).

**What is currently authored:** Full identity block, ecological rationale, and Level 1 and 2 protocol content are well developed. Level 3 protocol name and output metric are drafted. Equipment and references are present for Levels 1–2.

**What is missing:** A1 pressure linkages, A2 challenge linkages, B1/B2 practice linkages, Linkage C. Level 3 seasonal windows, equipment, and references. A4 EFG relevance list.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 2–3 hours to complete linkage blocks and Level 3 fields. Recommended reviewer: herpetologist or freshwater ecologist with agricultural water body experience.

## Profile 39 — Salamanders and Newts

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate in temperate regions where caudate amphibian species (newts, salamanders) are ecologically present. Requires presence of permanent or seasonal pond, ditch, or slow-flowing watercourse with confirmed or suspected historical breeding population.

**Ecological role:** Newts and salamanders are among the most sensitive indicators of pond and ditch habitat quality in European temperate agricultural landscapes. Great crested newt (*Triturus cristatus*) is a European Protected Species and a legal indicator of pond condition in EU-funded agri-environment schemes — making this indicator directly linked to compliance monitoring for European projects. Neotenic salamanders (axolotl relatives) fill the same role in Mediterranean dryland contexts. Amphibian community composition at farm ponds tracks pesticide drift, nutrient loading, and pond permanence more sensitively than any macroinvertebrate index.

**Relevant landscapes:** Northern and Western Europe (pond-rich farmland, riparian buffers), Central Europe, Mediterranean fringe with permanent water, temperate Central Asia. Not relevant to sub-Saharan Africa or arid deployments.

**What is currently authored:** Profile number, name, tier, conditionality criteria, hard prerequisite, and monitoring role. A brief equipment note (torch) appears in the Level 1 block. Everything else is missing.

**What is missing:** Category; ecological rationale (monitoring stage, response timescale); A1–A3 linkages; A4 EFGs; B1/B2 linkages; all three protocol levels in full; Linkage C; validation status.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 4–6 hours for complete from-scratch authoring. Recommended reviewer: amphibian ecologist with European agri-environment experience (Great Crested Newt monitoring standard is well-established and provides a ready framework).

## Profile 40 — Large Soil Invertebrates

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate in deployment contexts where large soil invertebrate communities — particularly millipedes, centipedes, scarab beetle larvae, and large diplopoda — are ecologically present and monitoring is specifically targeting soil engineering function beyond earthworms and termites.

**Ecological role:** Large soil invertebrates (body length >2cm, excluding earthworms, termites, and ants which have their own profiles) form a functionally distinct component of the soil macrofauna community in tropical and subtropical systems. They include scarab beetle larvae (chafer grubs, dung beetle larvae) that drive macropore formation; millipedes that process leaf litter and fix atmospheric nitrogen symbiotically; and centipedes as top predators of the soil mesofauna community. In systems where earthworm activity is limited by soil pH, texture, or climate, this group provides alternative metrics of soil biological function recovery under reduced tillage and organic matter management practices.

**Relevant landscapes:** Tropical and subtropical cropland, agroforestry, and pasture systems; particularly relevant in West and Central Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and Central America where earthworm densities are naturally low but large-bodied soil fauna are abundant.

**What is currently authored:** Profile number, name, tier, conditionality criteria, hard prerequisite, and monitoring role. Equipment notes appear in the Level 1 and 2 blocks (sorting tray, forceps, gloves). Everything else is missing.

**What is missing:** Category; ecological rationale; A1–A3 linkages; A4 EFGs; B1/B2 linkages; all three protocol levels in full; Linkage C.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 4–6 hours. Recommended reviewer: tropical soil ecologist; tropical agronomy specialist. Note: the Ecdysis 1000 Farms Protocol has a large soil invertebrate extraction component that could serve as the Level 1 standard method.

## Profile 41 — Crop Pest Invertebrates

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate in deployment contexts where economic crop pest invertebrate monitoring is required as part of integrated pest management (IPM) practice verification, and where pest pressure is sufficient to justify structured pest density monitoring.

**Ecological role:** This profile occupies a different niche from the other 40 profiles: it is primarily an **agricultural decision-support** indicator rather than a biodiversity indicator. Monitoring crop pest populations directly — aphids, thrips, white fly, stem borers, weevils depending on crop — provides the evidence base for verifying that practices such as reduced insecticide use (P23), habitat management for natural enemies (P11), and crop rotation (P07) are genuinely reducing pest pressure. It closes the causal loop between NBS practice adoption and the economic outcome that motivates farmer participation. Without this indicator, LAHMP cannot demonstrate that IPM practices are holding pest populations below economic thresholds, which is the single most important evidence gap for farmer buy-in and funder reporting.

**Relevant landscapes:** Any agricultural landscape where crop production is a primary livelihood and pest pressure is economically significant. Particularly important for annual cropland systems (T7.1), irrigated cropland, and mixed crop-livestock systems.

**What is currently authored:** Profile number, name, tier, conditionality criteria, hard prerequisite, and monitoring role. Equipment notes appear in the Level 1 block (hand lens, field datasheet, economic threshold tables). Everything else is missing.

**What is missing:** Category; ecological rationale; A1–A3 linkages; A4 EFGs; B1/B2 linkages; all three protocol levels in full; Linkage C.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 3–5 hours. Recommended reviewer: agronomist or IPM specialist. Note: FAO IPM field school methodology and CABI crop pest monitoring guides provide ready Level 1 and 2 frameworks that require minimal adaptation.
