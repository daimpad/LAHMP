### Profile 37 — Termites

**Status:** `populated: "draft"` — full protocol content from Word doc; A1/A2/B1/B2/A4 proposed from analogues

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate in tropical, subtropical, and semi-arid agricultural deployment contexts where termite activity is ecologically present and measurable.

**Ecological role:** Termites are the dominant soil engineers in tropical and subtropical agricultural landscapes. Fungus-growing and grass-harvesting colonies drive nutrient cycling, water infiltration, and organic matter decomposition at rates that can exceed earthworm activity in equivalent temperate systems. Mound-building species create persistent micro-habitat structures lasting decades. Termite mound density is a direct indicator of soil restoration under agroforestry, no-till, and organic matter management practices — making this profile especially relevant for Sahel, sub-Saharan Africa, and dry tropical Asia deployment contexts.

**Content from Word documents (complete):**
- Full identity block, ecological rationale (monitoring stage, response timescale)
- A3 services linkages: [15, 11] — soil fertility, food provision
- Level 1: Termite mound count transect and activity assessment (wet season timing, field datasheet equipment, Eggleton et al. 2002 reference)
- Level 2: Pitfall trap + soil core functional group assessment (morphospecies richness, functional group proportions)
- Level 3: Full community assessment with molecular ID and service quantification (infiltration factor, N-fixation rate)

**System-proposed by analogy (from Earthworms #06 and Ants #08):**
- A1 pressures: [1, 2, 4, 19, 20, 22] — tillage, compaction, insecticides, vegetation removal, wood extraction, land-use change
- A2 challenges: [1, 2, 4, 7, 21] — soil OM depletion, soil structure degradation, nutrient cycle disruption, soil biodiversity loss, macro-fauna loss
- A4 EFGs: T7.1, T7.2, T7.5
- B1: P01, P08, P13, P19
- B2: P08, P01 — agroforestry and no-till are the primary practices verified by termite mound recovery
- Level 2 seasonal window and references — extended from Level 1 context
- Level 3 seasonal window and equipment — proposed

**Fields requiring expert review:**
- A1/A2 pressure and challenge linkages (analogy-derived)
- B1/B2 practice assignments
- Level 2 and 3 seasonal windows
- Level 2 and 3 references (Jouquet et al. 2011 proposed but should be verified)
- Level 3 equipment list completeness

### Profile 38 — Freshwater Turtles

**Status:** `populated: "draft"` — full protocol content from Word doc; A1/A2/B1/B2/A4 proposed from analogues

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate where freshwater turtle species are ecologically present and regionally recognised as indicators of water body health. Requires confirmed presence of permanent water bodies (pond, river, irrigation canal, rice paddy system, or equivalent) within or adjacent to the monitored landscape.

**Ecological role:** Freshwater turtles are long-lived apex omnivores of agricultural water bodies. Their population structure integrates decades of water quality and habitat availability signals that shorter-lived species cannot provide. They are highly sensitive to pesticide bioaccumulation, organic pollution, water body permanence, and invasive species predation. Where rice-paddy and irrigation canal networks are involved — as in many IUCN NBS Hub contexts in Asia and Africa — turtle presence/absence is one of the most accessible indicators of water body ecological function for community monitors.

**Content from Word documents (complete):**
- Full identity block, ecological rationale (Slow Stage 3, Year 5–10+; medium 3–7 year response)
- A3 services linkages: [19, 6] — water quality, aquatic food provision
- Level 1: Basking count survey — binoculars, community observer method; warm season timing
- Level 2: Floating hoop trap survey — CPUE, size-class proportions, sex ratio; welfare notes (float collar mandatory)
- Level 3: Mark-recapture with PIT tagging and eDNA

**System-proposed by analogy (from Aquatic macroinvertebrates #17 and Fish #18):**
- A1 pressures: [17, 16, 15, 18, 4, 21] — water pollution, drainage, water abstraction, overgrazing, insecticides, invasive species
- A2 challenges: [17, 13, 14, 16] — water quality degradation, hydrological change, invasive species, aquatic habitat loss
- A4 EFGs: T7.1, T7.2, T7.5, F3.3 (rice paddies — critical for SE Asia/South Asia deployment)
- B1: P13, P16, P17, P19
- B2: P13, P16 — riparian buffers and wetland management are the primary practices verified by turtle population recovery
- Level 2 and 3 seasonal windows — extended from Level 1 context
- Level 3 equipment and references — proposed from standard herpetology methods

**Fields requiring expert review:**
- A1/A2 linkages (analogy-derived from aquatic invertebrate analogues — may need adjustment for vertebrate-specific pressures)
- B1/B2 practice assignments — particularly B2 (which practices specifically use turtle surveys as primary evidence?)
- Level 2 reference completeness (Gibbons & Semlitsch 1982 is foundational but regional guides may be more appropriate)
- Level 3 equipment list — PIT tagging welfare requirements vary by jurisdiction

### Profile 39 — Salamanders and Newts

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate in temperate regions where caudate amphibian species are ecologically present. Requires permanent or seasonal pond, ditch, or slow-flowing watercourse with confirmed or suspected historical breeding population.

**Ecological role:** Newts and salamanders are among the most sensitive indicators of pond and ditch habitat quality in European temperate agricultural landscapes. Great crested newt (*Triturus cristatus*) is a European Protected Species and a legal indicator in EU agri-environment schemes — making this profile directly relevant for compliance monitoring in European projects. Amphibian community composition tracks pesticide drift, nutrient loading, and pond permanence more sensitively than any macroinvertebrate index.

**What is currently authored:** Profile number, name, tier, conditionality criteria, hard prerequisite, monitoring role. Equipment note in Level 1 block (torch). Category, ecological rationale, all linkages, and all protocol content are absent.

**Recommended reviewer:** Amphibian ecologist with European agri-environment experience. The UK Great Crested Newt eDNA standard method (Natural England, 2014) provides a ready Level 1/2 framework.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 4–6 hours.

### Profile 40 — Large Soil Invertebrates

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate where large soil invertebrate communities (body length >2cm) — particularly millipedes, centipedes, and scarab beetle larvae — are ecologically present and monitoring targets soil engineering function beyond earthworms and termites.

**Ecological role:** This group forms a functionally distinct component of tropical/subtropical soil macrofauna. Millipedes drive leaf litter decomposition; centipedes are top predators of the soil mesofauna community; scarab larvae drive macropore formation. In systems where earthworm activity is limited by soil pH, texture, or climate, this group provides alternative metrics of soil biological function recovery under reduced tillage and organic matter management practices. Particularly relevant for West and Central Africa, South and Southeast Asia deployment contexts.

**What is currently authored:** Profile number, name, tier, conditionality criteria, hard prerequisite, monitoring role. Equipment notes in Level 1/2 blocks (sorting tray, forceps, gloves). Everything else absent.

**Recommended reviewer:** Tropical soil ecologist. The Ecdysis 1000 Farms Protocol has a large soil invertebrate extraction component that could serve as the Level 1 standard method.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 4–6 hours.

### Profile 41 — Crop Pest Invertebrates

**Conditionality criteria:** Activate where economic crop pest monitoring is required as part of IPM practice verification, and where pest pressure is sufficient to justify structured monitoring.

**Ecological role:** This is primarily an **agricultural decision-support** indicator. Monitoring crop pest populations (aphids, thrips, white fly, stem borers, weevils) provides the evidence base for verifying that IPM practices are genuinely reducing pest pressure. It closes the causal loop between NBS adoption and the economic outcome that motivates farmer participation — without this profile, LAHMP cannot demonstrate that practices are holding pest populations below economic thresholds. Especially important for farmer buy-in and funder impact reporting.

**What is currently authored:** Profile number, name, tier, conditionality criteria, hard prerequisite, monitoring role. Equipment note in Level 1 block. Everything else absent.

**Recommended reviewer:** Agronomist or IPM specialist. FAO IPM Field School methodology and CABI crop pest monitoring guides provide ready Level 1/2 frameworks.

**Estimated authoring effort:** 3–5 hours.
